Norway's Hidden Livestock

Nearly 900 million farmed salmon are swimming in Norwegian waters right now. That's more than the entire human population of Europe.

By comparison, Norway's entire land-based livestock totals roughly 12.3 million poultry, 1.5 million sheep, 850,000 cattle, and 750,000 pigs.

Farmed salmon outnumber poultry — Norway's most populous land animal — by more than a factor of 70.

92.5% of all production animals in Norway are farmed salmon. Including trout and cod raises the total share of farmed fish to over 98%.

Put another way: with 5.5 million people, every Norwegian is effectively responsible for 164 farmed salmon right now — and about 36 salmon deaths per person per year.

Making It Real

Numbers in the hundreds of millions are hard to grasp. Let's put them in perspective.

900 million salmon is 165 times Norway's entire human population.

The 200 million salmon that die each year roughly equals the entire population of Brazil.

If you counted one salmon per second, non-stop, it would take 28 years to count all the farmed salmon in Norway.

Share of global farmed salmon production

The World's Largest Producer

Norway dominates global salmon farming, controlling more than half of the world's total production. That scale demands extraordinary responsibility.

Global farmed salmon production reached 2.9 million tonnes in 2023. Norway alone accounted for over 53%. Chile follows at roughly 25%.

This dominance means Norway has a unique responsibility — and unique power — to set welfare standards for the entire global industry.

The Wild vs Farmed Divide

Norway is famous for its wild salmon rivers. But the balance between wild and farmed has shifted beyond recognition.

Only about 400,000 wild Atlantic salmon return to Norwegian rivers each year — down from 530,000 in 2010. Meanwhile, 884 million farmed salmon swim in sea pens.

Farmed salmon outnumber wild salmon by approximately 2,200 to 1.

The Death Toll

In 2023, around 200 million Norwegian farmed salmon died during production — before ever reaching slaughter.

That number alone exceeds the total of every other animal used in Norwegian food production combined.

In the same year, about 5.4 million poultry died during production.

240,000 pigs and 100,000 small ruminants also died.

Behind these figures lies tremendous pain, disease, and poor living conditions. Common causes of death include winter ulcers, heart failure, and gill disorders.

A Growing Crisis

This is not a new problem — and it's not getting better. Despite growing awareness and industry pledges, the numbers remain stubbornly high.

The Office of the Auditor General criticised the authorities in 2019 for inadequate follow-up. A second investigation in 2023 found the same persistent challenges.

Year after year, the industry grows — but welfare improvements have not kept pace with production expansion.

Mortality rate before slaughter

The Highest Mortality Rate

Farmed salmon also has the highest mortality rate among all animals raised for food in Norway.

The Norwegian Veterinary Institute reports a 16.7% mortality rate for farmed salmon in the seawater phase — meaning roughly one in six salmon die before slaughter.

That's higher than pigs (12%), turkeys (9%), or cattle (8%). The mortality rate is often the consequence of prolonged pain, illness, stress, and unfavourable living conditions.

Where the losses happen

Where They Die

Problems start at the hatchery (smolt production) stage, before salmon are placed in sea pens. These facilities often feature elevated water temperatures, intense lighting, and high tank densities.

Approximately 60 million salmon die in the smolt phase, and an additional 80 million are destroyed.

Things do not get better once the salmon reach the sea. An additional 60 million fish die during the seawater phase, primarily from infectious diseases.

Disease and Suffering

Diseases in the seawater phase are grave and painful. Winter ulcers, caused by bacteria, are among the most common — producing large, open wounds on the fish.

In 2023, winter ulcers were recorded at 320 of 815 active seawater production sites — despite these conditions not even being subject to mandatory reporting.

Heart diseases like CMS (cardiomyopathy syndrome) damage the heart and lead to acute heart failure. Gill diseases further weaken the fish. High cage density increases stress and makes fish more vulnerable to illness.

Cleaner fish deployed yearly

The Cleaner Fish Tragedy

Salmon lice are among the main threats to salmon welfare at sea. To combat them, roughly 30 million cleaner fish are deployed yearly in Norway. These smaller fish eat lice off salmon.

But the approach causes considerable suffering. More than 40% of cleaner fish die. This can hardly be consistent with the Animal Welfare Act, which states that animals have intrinsic value.

Major producer SalMar has already stated it will no longer rely on cleaner fish — a positive development. But without this method, the need for alternative lice treatments grows.

Thermal Delousing

Thermal delousing involves immersing salmon in 28–34°C water for up to 30 seconds. For context, salmon thrive at 8–14°C. This is, in effect, a torture chamber for fish.

Research shows this triggers intense pain and stress reactions. Salmon exhibit panic behaviour with violent movements and sometimes collide with surrounding equipment. Some lose consciousness from the heat.

Physical injuries such as wounds, fin erosion, and eye damage are common. The Norwegian Food Safety Authority reports high mortality rates during and after thermal delousing.

Norway's own Animal Welfare Act (2009) states that animals — including fish — "shall be protected against unnecessary danger of suffering." Thermal delousing appears difficult to reconcile with this law.

Five Steps Forward

The question is no longer whether there is an animal welfare crisis in Norwegian aquaculture — but how to address it. Here are five proposed measures.

01

Ban cleaner fish and thermal delousing

Both methods inflict severe suffering on fish. Invest instead in humane alternatives: closed-containment pens, improved feed, genetic breeding for lice resistance, and laser technology.

02

Halt production if mortality is too high

Expand Norway's existing "traffic-light system" to include farmed salmon welfare. If mortality rates exceed sustainable levels, authorities should have the power to scale back production.

03

Introduce a tax on suffering

Reform the tax system so producers with poor animal welfare standards are penalised, while those who treat animals well are rewarded. The system can be designed revenue-neutral.

04

Strengthen the regulatory authority

The Norwegian Food Safety Authority lacks sufficient resources. It must be bolstered, and fines for breaches of the Animal Welfare Act must be substantially increased.

05

Require less intensive smolt production

Reduce hatchery growth rates and replicate natural lighting and temperature patterns. A more robust smolt enhances welfare across its entire lifespan.

This crisis demands action

Norway controls over half of the world's farmed salmon production. We have a special responsibility — and the power to lead change.
